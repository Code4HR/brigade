Meet Spike, OpenOakland Captain

What we’re doing is tech-focused, but also social change work. If you want to build cool stuff, then do that, but opening government and providing technology that makes a difference for our communities means a lot of vulnerability and humility will be required...

... you'll get stuff wrong, make poor assumptions and build things that don't have all the success you'd like. But this all serves to grow you and your organization to do things better and may force you to listen and learn and refocus on things that matter.

How It All Began

In 2012, the Captains (Spike (above) and Eddie (below)) originally met through the Code for America network, saying they chose each other as mentors, and began collaborating to do work in the city they both love. They believe strongly in the Code for America model for transforming local government and want to bring it to a local level in Oakland. They began by hosting hackathons, which they found were good for building community, but not for building sustainable technology. While continuing to host larger events, they have also started a more regular meeting model. OpenOakland began by meeting monthly, then biweekly, and now weekly.

OpenOakland first met at the Urban Strategies Council offices, then the Oakland Tribune offices, but when Nicole Neiditch, former Online Engagement Manager for the City of Oakland, now Code for America Fellowship Director, got involved she invited the group to City Hall. They now meet every Tuesday at 6:30 in a hearing room on the first floor of City Hall. They’re still finding a balance of getting participation from city staff with meeting at a time that’s after work hours. There are a few city staff that regularly attend, but even without a huge amount of city staff participation, they’re finding that social awareness is helping to build support.

In late 2013, OpenOakland members asked the organization for more leadership capacity to take on all of the opportunities presented to the group. Choosing participants who consistently engaged, OpenOakland formed a core leadership team of approximately 20 volunteers.

They meet Tuesdays, at 6:30pm in a Hearing Room on the first floor of City Hall.
